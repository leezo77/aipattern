The AI-Ready Organization

A Maturity Framework for Roles & Personas. Understand who to engage at each stage of the AI adoption journey.

It's Not Just About Technology, It's About People

An organization's AI maturity isn't just defined by its technology stack; it's reflected in its team structure and the roles it cultivates. As a company progresses from simple experiments (Crawl) to integrated solutions (Walk) and finally to strategic, autonomous systems (Run), the key personas you need to engage with will change. This guide identifies who these people are, what they care about, and how to build a maturity framework that includes not just the solution, but the team behind it.

Crawl: Exploration & Enablement

The organization is in an experimental phase, focused on low-risk, high-visibility projects to prove the value of AI. The goal is to learn, build foundational skills, and identify viable use cases. Teams are often informal and cross-functional.

The Business Analyst / Data Analyst

A data-savvy professional from a business unit who understands a specific domain's challenges and data assets. They are tasked with finding opportunities for AI.

Primary Focus:

  • Identifying high-value, low-complexity use cases.
  • Understanding and preparing data for initial PoCs.
  • Measuring the business impact of pilot projects.

How to Engage Them:

With practical, easy-to-understand tools and success stories relevant to their specific business domain.

The Citizen Developer

A tech-curious business user empowered by low-code/no-code platforms. They build simple applications and automations to solve immediate problems for their team.

Primary Focus:

  • Rapidly building simple AI-powered apps (e.g., RAG chatbots).
  • Automating repetitive tasks within their department.
  • Demonstrating quick wins with tangible results.

How to Engage Them:

With no-code tools like AI Agent Builder, clear tutorials, and a focus on speed-to-value.

Walk: Operationalization & Scaling

The organization has seen initial success and is now focused on building robust, scalable AI solutions for core business processes. This phase is about creating dedicated teams, formalizing processes, and moving from PoCs to production systems.

The AI Engineer

A specialized software engineer who designs, builds, and deploys production-grade AI systems. They bridge the gap between ML models and scalable software applications.

Primary Focus:

  • Integrating AI models into business applications via APIs.
  • Building data pipelines and agentic reasoning loops.
  • Ensuring solutions are scalable, reliable, and secure.

How to Engage Them:

With low-code/pro-code tools like Agent Workbench, robust APIs, and clear architectural patterns.

The MLOps Engineer

A role that combines Machine Learning, DevOps, and Data Engineering. They are responsible for automating the deployment, monitoring, and management of the ML model lifecycle.

Primary Focus:

  • Building CI/CD pipelines for models and AI applications.
  • Monitoring model performance, drift, and cost in production.
  • Ensuring the entire AI system is observable and reliable.

How to Engage Them:

With platforms that offer built-in observability, versioning, and automated deployment capabilities.

The Prompt Engineer / Conversation Designer

A specialist who crafts, refines, and optimizes the natural language prompts used to guide AI models. They ensure the AI's responses are accurate, helpful, and on-brand.

Primary Focus:

  • Designing effective prompt chains and agent instructions.
  • Testing and evaluating the quality of LLM outputs.
  • Curating datasets for fine-tuning models.

How to Engage Them:

With tools that provide playgrounds for prompt testing, version control for prompts, and detailed analytics on AI performance.

The AI Governance & Ethics Specialist

A role focused on ensuring that AI systems are developed and used responsibly. They establish policies for fairness, transparency, privacy, and compliance.

Primary Focus:

  • Conducting bias and fairness audits on models.
  • Ensuring compliance with data privacy regulations (GDPR, etc.).
  • Defining guidelines for responsible AI use.

How to Engage Them:

With platforms that provide transparency, explainability features, and tools for monitoring bias and data leakage.

Run: Strategic Autonomy & Transformation

AI is deeply integrated into the enterprise strategy and is driving business transformation. The focus is on building complex, multi-agent systems, fostering a culture of AI-driven innovation, and managing a portfolio of intelligent solutions.

The AI Strategist / Chief AI Officer (CAIO)

A senior leader responsible for the enterprise-wide AI strategy. They align AI initiatives with business goals and oversee the entire AI portfolio.

Primary Focus:

  • Defining the vision for AI across the organization.
  • Securing budget and resources for large-scale AI projects.
  • Managing the portfolio of AI solutions to maximize ROI.
  • Building an AI-first culture.

How to Engage Them:

With strategic frameworks, total cost of ownership (TCO) models, and case studies demonstrating transformational business impact.

The AI Architect

The senior technical authority responsible for designing the overall architecture for complex, multi-agent AI systems and ensuring they integrate with the enterprise ecosystem.

Primary Focus:

  • Designing scalable, resilient, and secure AI platforms.
  • Selecting the right combination of models, tools, and frameworks.
  • Governing the technical standards for all AI development.

How to Engage Them:

With comprehensive platform documentation, performance benchmarks, and detailed architectural blueprints.

Evolving Foundational Roles

Some roles are present across all phases, but their focus and sophistication grow with the organization's maturity.

The AI Product Manager

Owns the 'what' and 'why' of an AI product. Their role evolves from managing single features to overseeing complex, autonomous systems.

  • Crawl: Defines requirements for simple PoCs.
  • Walk: Manages the roadmap for a production AI application.
  • Run: Manages a portfolio of interconnected AI agents and systems.

The Solution Architect

Designs how the AI solution fits within the broader enterprise IT landscape. Their scope expands from single applications to entire platforms.

  • Crawl: Designs the integration for a single AI PoC.
  • Walk: Architects a scalable AI service for a business unit.
  • Run: Designs the enterprise-wide AI platform and data fabric.
